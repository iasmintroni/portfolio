Exploring Rammed Earth Construction for Sustainable Architecture

I explored rammed earth as a sustainable construction material, focusing on techniques, applications, and alternatives for earth walls. Inspired by Maggie’s Centres, my project proposed a community space in Hebden Bridge. Model-making revealed that replacing cement with Pulverized Fuel Ash (PFA) enhances sustainability, with an optimal mix of 15-20% clay, 5-10% silt, and 75% sand. I successfully demonstrated moulding rammed earth into shapes and constructing inclined walls using site-specific materials. This research highlights rammed earth’s potential as an eco-friendly building solution, contributing to resilient and sustainable architectural practices.

The Untold Presence of the Absence.

I embarked on a journey inspired by a visit to the Jewish Museum of Lecce, where the sight of an ornate column in the courtyard of Palazzo Personè—once home to the main synagogue—sparked my curiosity. This encounter led me to question the untold and the abstract; it led me to a quest of breathing life into what has been erased and to reveal the past through what still lingers.

Drawn into the intricate nature of architectural ornaments, I began to question their existence and explore the hidden intentions behind their creation. This dissertation sought to understand the perception of absence, where the unseen is made tangible through the presence of ornamentation. Through carefully crafted sentences, paragraphs, and arguments, I sought to unearth the stories that have faded from view, illuminating the concealed narratives of history.

This research has profoundly influenced my research methodology. By looking into the past of the site, I have sought to craft a design that grounds itself in history, allowing the echoes of the past to inform and shape the present.

About the model

This project draws inspiration from the site’s historical significance and the passage project, utilising the interplay of shadows and light to create a captivating facade. It integrates elements from the past and present, establishing a communal space for contemplation and interaction. The design showcases a poetic, cave-like aesthetic with rhythmic walls, prominently featuring rammed earth as a central element. Each building within the project serves a unique purpose while harmoniously coexisting on a shared roof, embodying unity and the collective strength of the alliance. Together, they form a diverse and harmonious collection of buildings.

Software used: AUTOCAD, RHIINO, PHOTOSHOP

The design integrates elements from both history and modernity, creating a space for communal reflection and interaction. The rhythmic, cave-like walls of rammed earth embody a poetic aesthetic, where each structure serves a unique purpose while coexisting under a shared roof, symbolising unity and collective strength. The “ghosts” of the past leave invisible traces in the architecture, radiating from sources of trauma and lingering as absences that are inscribed in the very fabric of the site. Together, these elements form a diverse yet harmonious collection of buildings, uniting the community in a shared journey of healing and remembrance.

The West’s Future Food

Location: Fowey, Cornwall, England

In this project I combined architecture and gastronomy to address the challenges of population growth and sluggish food production. It presents a dual-purpose establishment—a restaurant and a jellyfish production building—where sustainable jellyfish becomes the alternative food source. By advocating dietary changes and promoting sustainable practices, this project contributes to securing a brighter future for Earth and its inhabitants.
